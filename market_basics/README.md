Here I want to address a phenomenon I see often among newer participants.
Some more advanced people fall into this group as well, but far less often than those who are just starting out.

The pattern is usually this: you understand mathematics, computer science, and pattern prediction, but you do not yet understand trading. You arrive at the challenge hoping that A can simply be deduced from B. In reality, that is often not the case. Fortunately, almost everything can be studied and learned today.

I want to explain trading in a slightly controversial way, because I think it helps people understand their role more clearly.

For the sake of the story, imagine that I am a trader, not a quant trader, just a trader in the older, more traditional sense. Many game theory questions and economic thought experiments revolve around what it means to be a trader, from the travelling salesman problem to basic reflections on supply and demand.

A trader, in the ordinary sense, is simply someone who trades: fish for eggs, linen for coins, and so on. My own first exposure to this idea came from World of Warcraft, where I traded linen and wool cloth for copper coins. Some traders realise early that specialising in a particular market can be highly beneficial. Building a monopoly is difficult, but when it happens, it can be extremely rewarding.

In that same spirit, what we are really doing is building a system that responds to demand with supply. We function as agents of supply and demand, matching the two at a price. With that in mind, here are three useful ways to think about trading.

### Supply and demand — the trader’s role

A trader is first and foremost a key part of the market. Our job is to meet and match supply and demand.

Successful traders find good matches between supply and demand and keep a profit, however small, for themselves. Great traders go further: they anticipate swings in supply and demand and position themselves to profit from those changes over time.

That is also what is happening in modern trading. Despite the obsession with price charts, price itself is only an aggregated expression of supply and demand. That is why IMC gives us order books. In the simplest possible terms, we are looking at incoming bid orders, meaning buy orders or demand, and ask orders, meaning sell orders or supply.

Our role, then, is to act as agents of the market, matching those two sides not only right now, but also with some view of what is likely to happen next.

To become genuinely good traders, we therefore need to anticipate future supply and demand. This is where ideas such as momentum, trends, and mean reversion start to make sense. These are not abstract buzzwords. They are phenomena that any fish trader, grain merchant, or market vendor could experience.

A bad season might reduce supply and push prices higher. That would appear as a trend.
A stable yearly cycle might produce low demand in summer and high demand in winter. That would resemble seasonality or mean reversion.
If a certain type of fish were becoming scarce near our harbour town, prices might suddenly become unstable because nobody is quite sure how much supply remains. That would look like a volatility spike.

All of these things affect not only price, but also the underlying supply and demand that create price. That is why we care about fair value, about when something is cheap enough to buy, about how much inventory we want to hold for tomorrow or next week, and about temporary dislocations. There may be moments during a broader uptrend where prices briefly flatten because one fisherman brings in an unusually large catch, even though prices are likely to rise again next week.

### Supply and demand — arbitrage and advantages

As a trader, this type of situation comes up again and again.

Imagine I am a fish trader in the town market. By chance, I become close friends with an older fisherman who lives just down the beach. Years ago, he found a pirate treasure and now fishes purely for fun. Because of our friendship, he lets me take 90% of his catch every day for $1. The next day, I bring that fish into town and sell it for $100.

This is a fantastic opportunity. In essence, I make an enormous return. The other traders in town do not have this advantage, either because they do not know about the fisherman or because they lack the means to get to him. In trading terms, this is a form of arbitrage: I have access to a source of value that others do not, and that gives me a significant edge.

A similar example from World of Warcraft was the commodity stranglekelp, an important ingredient in various crafted items. By chance, I discovered an area rich in stranglekelp that most players did not know about. That gave me an advantage similar to the old fisherman giving away nearly free fish. If stranglekelp sold for 1000 copper coins per piece and was scarce in the broader market, then access to that hidden supply was extremely valuable.

The broader point is simple: edges often come from access, information, logistics, or speed. Not every advantage is some elaborate mathematical insight. Sometimes it is just knowing where the cheap fish are.

### Supply and demand — the marketplace and competitors

Many people ask, quite reasonably, what to do about competitors in the marketplace.

At the moment, in the tutorial round, there are no true competitors. We only have customers. That means it is not especially useful to spend time trying to psychoanalyse customer behaviour. A fish trader would not stand in his little shop trying to reverse-engineer every customer’s personality. He would put out prices in the morning and observe what happens.

If your only goal is to attract more customers, then the solution is obvious: lower your prices. That is what any ordinary shop would do. But if your goal is to maximise profit over time, then the question changes. Now you care about what prices allow you to earn the most overall, not simply how many people buy from you. That is where fair value matters.

This leads to the question many people keep repeating: the informed trader.

As the name suggests, the informed trader is a competitor in the marketplace who has more information than we do. It is worth noting that most anxiety around this trader comes from round 5 of IMC Prosperity 3. After reading Frankfurt Hedgehogs’ submissions, many people begin worrying about how to respond to an informed trader, even though that trader does not exist in the current setting.

We can imagine, in a slightly silly but useful way, that the informed trader has a little spy niece or nephew running around town the night before, asking key customers what price they would be willing to trade at tomorrow. Whether the informed trader knows future prices more accurately or simply understands customer behaviour better is not the important part. The important part is that they possess an information advantage.

If that kind of competitor exists, then of course we must respond. We should not simply allow them to exploit that advantage and take our business. One very simple, slightly childish, response would be to move one step behind them until we learn enough to keep up or beat them. It is not elegant, but it captures the idea that competition forces adaptation.

This example appears again and again in real markets. IMC did not choose it by accident. Any real trader understands that there is always competition, and there will always be someone smarter, faster, or better informed than you in some dimension. That is not an exception. That is the nature of markets.
