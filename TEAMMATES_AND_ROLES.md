Before you start advertising yourself, or before you start reaching out to others, it helps to think a bit about what teams actually need.

In practice, many teams end up resembling a small multi-strategy trading shop. That does not mean everyone needs a fancy title, but it does help to understand the kinds of roles that naturally emerge.

A typical strong team often needs some mixture of the following:

- **Developer** — builds and maintains the codebase, handles structure, implementation, testing, and general system reliability
- **Researcher** — explores patterns, designs signals, tests ideas, and improves specific parts of the strategy
- **Trader** — thinks about execution, behaviour, market logic, and how strategy decisions translate into PnL
- **Analyst** — evaluates results, interprets data, identifies weaknesses, and helps connect findings back into the system
- **Portfolio / team lead** — keeps the bigger picture in mind, decides where effort should go, and makes sure the whole setup is moving in a sensible direction

In a small team, one person may cover more than one of these roles. But before looking for teammates, it is worth asking yourself:

**What role am I strongest in?**  
**What role do I actually want to take on?**  
**What kind of people would complement me well?**

## Find Teammates on the Discord Channel

Once you understand the basic team structure, it becomes much easier to advertise yourself well.

Instead of just writing “I study maths” or “I know Python,” try to communicate:
- what your background is
- what you are actually good at
- how much time you can commit
- what kind of teammate or team you are looking for

For individuals looking to join a team, a message like this usually works well:

```text
Hi everyone, I'm [name / short intro].

My background is in [degree / field / work area]. I have experience with [key skills: ML, Python, backtesting, trading systems, optimisation, statistics, etc.].

So far I have worked on [brief examples: algos, models, research projects, competitions, hackathons, portfolio optimisation, RL environments, pricing tools, etc.]. My strongest areas are [2–4 strengths].

For Prosperity, I am [first-timer / returning participant], and I’m planning to commit [time level: a few hours a day / part-time / close to full-time during rounds]. My goal is [learn a lot / build strong systems / place highly / aim for top global ranking].

I’m looking for teammates who are [serious / curious / technically strong / experienced / collaborative]. It would be especially great to team up with people who bring strength in [trading intuition / quant research / ML / coding / maths / statistics / execution / teamwork].

Feel free to DM me if this sounds like a good fit.
```

The main point is to tell people not just what you have studied, but what you can contribute.

## What Teams Are Usually Looking For

Without overcomplicating it, most teams are usually looking for some combination of the following.

### Developer

A strong developer should be comfortable coding, ideally in Python, and should be able to work with a somewhat object-oriented setup, since that is often how the IMC framework is structured.

It is also useful if they have some experience with:

- debugging
- data handling
- basic data engineering
- backtesting infrastructure
- making systems easier to maintain and improve

### Researcher

A researcher should be able to deep dive into a problem and come back with something useful.

That might mean:

- improving trend detection
- testing mean reversion ideas
- studying market behaviour
- comparing alternative signals
- validating whether an idea is actually robust

A good researcher does not just generate ideas. They investigate them properly.

### Trader

A trader-type teammate should have some intuition for markets, behaviour, execution, and profit generation.

They do not need to be a professional trader, but they should be able to think about questions such as:

why this strategy should make money
how inventory and execution interact
what kinds of market behaviours are likely being exploited
whether the system makes practical sense

### Analyst

An analyst should be able to inspect data, evaluate results, and connect conclusions back into the strategy.

This includes things like:

- spotting weaknesses in performance
- identifying when results are fragile
- interpreting patterns in PnL or behaviour
- helping the team understand what is and is not working

### Team lead / portfolio thinker

Someone also needs to keep track of the bigger picture.

This does not have to be a formal “portfolio manager,” but it is often useful to have one person asking:

- where should the team spend its time?
- which ideas are worth pursuing?
- which improvements matter most?
- how do all these pieces fit together into one stronger system?

## Team Leaders May Advertise Like This

Teams that already have a few members will often post messages describing their current composition and what they are missing.

For example:
```
We are looking for [number] more teammate(s) for Prosperity.

Current team:
- [member 1: school / rank / field / strength]
- [member 2: school / work / field / strength]
- [member 3: school / achievement / strength]

We are looking for people who are strong in [math / ML / coding / trading / quant research / backtesting / statistics] and are serious about contributing during the competition.

DM if interested.
```

When you read these messages, try to interpret them in terms of team roles.

If they are asking for coding or system-building skills, they are probably looking for a **developer**.

If they are asking for statistics, maths, signals, or modelling, they may be looking for a **researcher** or **analyst**.

If they are asking for trading intuition, execution ideas, or market understanding, they may be looking for a **trader-type** teammate.

So when you reply, it is often better to say:

**“I think I would fit your researcher role because…”**
or
**“I can contribute mainly on the development side…”**

rather than just listing degrees or subjects you have studied.


## Remote vs In-Person Teams

Remote teams can absolutely work. Different time zones can even be helpful, since people can keep making progress across more hours of the day.

That said, if you have the option, there is a real advantage to working in the same environment for at least some of the competition.

Being physically together can improve:

- focus
- speed of communication
- idea sharing
- debugging
- general team energy

Sometimes a shared table, a few hours of focused work, and a cup of coffee do more for a team than a long Discord thread ever will.

So remote is fine, but proximity can be a real advantage.

## Final Advice

When looking for teammates, try to think in terms of team fit, not just credentials.

Do not only ask:
- Do they study maths?
- Do they know machine learning?
- Do they have finance experience?

Also ask:
- What role would they actually play?
- What can they build, test, or improve?
- How serious are they?
- Will they communicate well?
- Do they complement the current team?

In general, it is much more useful to describe yourself and others in terms of contribution than in terms of labels alone.
